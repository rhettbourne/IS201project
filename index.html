"Six Seasons and a Movie"

"Jeff Winger is disbarred and suspended from his law firm when it is discovered that he lied about having a bachelor's degree from Columbia University. To earn a legitimate degree, he enrolls at Greendale Community College in Colorado. He quickly becomes attracted to his classmate, social activist Britta Perry, and pretends to run a study group in order to spend time with her. Britta invites classmate Abed Nadir, a socially awkward and pop culture obsessed student, who in turn brings other classmates along: religious single mother Shirley Bennett; naïve over-achiever Annie Edison; former high school football star Troy Barnes; and bigoted, elderly millionaire Pierce Hawthorne. Despite their differences, the group's members soon become close friends. "

In 2009, NBC was looking for a fourth sitcom to join The Office, 30 Rock, and Parks & Recreation on Thursday nights, hoping to recapture the formula of four half-hour comedies followed by a drama that had carried them for decades. This is where Dan Harmon’s Community came into play: a sitcom inspired by Harmon’s time at Glendale Community College and his relationship with a Spanish study group. The show started off as a more traditional sitcom, but increasing meta influences and homages/parodies led Community to develop a new style that was completely different from anything else on air. Though Community consistently struggled with viewers, it was acclaimed by critics and gained a large cult following. Due to the unique and ambitious nature of the show (and its refusal to appeal to wider audiences by going mainstream), Community constantly struggled. More than once, the show was delayed or cancelled, and more than once, fans came together to keep it alive. “Six seasons and a movie” (a running gag) soon became Community’s rallying cry.

Seasons 2 and 3 are widely considered to be the best of Community. Firing on all cylinders, these seasons are clever, heartfelt, and full of the magic that makes Community shine. Though Seasons 1, 5, and 6 are incredible, they each seem to be missing something. Season 1 can feel mainstream, and certain members of the study group seem out of character at times. Seasons 5 and 6 have their highs, but they suffer from the absence of Chevy Chase and Donald Glover, and have a different tone. Behind-the-scenes conflicts cut Dan Harmon from Season 4, before his eventual return for the final two seasons. Full of great ideas but executed poorly, Season 4 can feel hollow and is seen as the show’s low point.

Creating a definitive ranking for Community's 110 episodes would be impossible, as their appeal depends largely on the audience. However, the following list sorts out a general trend, with the best of Community at the top, and some of the show's low points at the bottom. The S-tier is made up of three near-perfect episodes. These episodes have incredible attention to detail, packed dialogue, and don't waste a second of the 22 minutes that they work with. They can (and should be) be watched with anyone, as they don't require too much context or parody a particular genre. The A-tier showcases Community's magic. These 11 episodes see the show at its best with great homages, heartfelt messages, and hysterical moments that defined Community. The B-tier has 21 solid episodes. These are good episodes that are held back in small ways (lackluster B-plots, uncomfortable moments, etc.). The C-tier is special. While it has many of Community's best episodes, it isn't for every audience. These 16 episodes are extra meta, take a particular concept to an extreme, require context that not all viewers have, or are just weird. The D-tier has 59 episodes that are missing something, ranging from good to embarrassing. Lumping the Pilot together with Intro to Felt Surrogacy feels wrong, but there's no clear spot to draw a line.

Season 3 - Episode 4

At Abed and Troy's housewarming party, Jeff decides to let the decision on who gets the pizza rest on the roll of the dice, leaving Abed to contemplate six alternate realities.

Widely regarded as the best episode of Community and arguably one of the best TV episodes of all time, Remedial Chaos Theory runs through the same scenario seven times (pulling a different member from the study group for each). There isn’t a wasted second, line of dialogue, or background detail (room number reference, Shirley’s apron, rum label, vending machine fridge, etc.). Hilarious and heartfelt.

Season 5 - Episode 4

After Pierce's unexpected passing, a team of investigators headed by the no-nonsense Mr. Stone arrives at Greendale to subject the study group to lie detector tests before they can be considered for distributions under the will.

Chosen for a fundraiser table read during COVID (that swapped Walton Goggins for Pedro Pascal), Cooperative Polygraphy is the last episode that captures the dynamic between the original seven members of the study group. Though Chevy Chase left the show at the end of S4, the writing makes it seem as though Pierce is still there; every line is gold.

Season 2 - Episode 8

Chaos and mistrust tear the study group apart as they try to figure out who stole Annie's pen, causing them to miss the Greendale Puppy Parade.

Cooperative Calligraphy was the episode that cemented the study group’s characters (Britta and Troy might as well have been different characters in the pilot), the dynamic between them, and their tendency to blow small situations out of proportion. The episode just gets better when you realize that Troy’s monkey can be seen stealing the pen at the beginning, and that “Gwynnifer” (a real-life person with whom Dan Harmon had a Twitter feud) is Jeff’s codename for Britta.

Season 2 - Episode 14

The study group plays Dungeons & Dragons with another classmate to improve his spirits, but Pierce's jealousies cause their good intentions to backfire.

Famous for being banned on Netflix and Hulu (Chang paints himself black), Advanced Dungeons & Dragons is Dan Harmon’s favorite episode. Embracing the playful, creative, and sometimes absurd nature of roleplaying games, it’s amazing that the episode can be so engaging without any visuals accompanying the characters’ actions.

Season 2 - Episode 23

In a "spaghetti western" parody, Pierce tries to get revenge on the rest of the study group during this year's paintball tournament.

Arguably the best of Community’s famous paintball episodes (and a perfect homage to spaghetti westerns with a cameo appearance by Lost’s Josh Holloway), A Fistful of Paintballs is the episode that led Marvel president Kevin Feige to give the Russo brothers the MCU’s reins. This is likely why Abed, Chang, Shirley, and Dean Pelton can be seen in the MCU (honorable mention for Brie Larson).

Season 3 - Episode 14

The students of Greendale are drawn into an epic pillow fight as Troy and Abed start a war that threatens to destroy their unique friendship.

A spoof on Ken Burns documentaries, Pillows and Blankets is narrated by Keith David (who has done narration for past Ken Burns documentaries and later stars as Elroy Patashnik in S6). The episode is equal parts ridiculous and wholesome, touching on Troy and Abed’s relationship, and Jeff’s growth as a character.

Season 2 - Episode 9

Dean Pelton tries to bust Jeff for a phony night school credit, which only unveils a series of conspiracies, plots, and double-crosses between Jeff, Annie, and the dean.

A parody of conspiracy films where the betrayals just keep overtaking one another, Conspiracy Theories and Interior Design is Community gold. As Jeff, Annie, Abed, and Troy chase Professor Professorson through a blanket fort, they are cut off by a crossing Latvian Independence Parade. The episode aired on November 18th, the day of the declaration of Latvian independence.

Season 5 - Episode 3

The college is imbued with panic when an unidentified person returns to dropping coins down people's butts and Annie takes it upon herself to find out who is behind it all.

Basic Intergluteal Numismatics is a perfect homage to David Fincher thrillers (such as Zodiac or Se7en) in music, lighting, style, and more. The episode brings back the ACB (mentioned in S2.E17), features an original song by Ben Folds (that’s a bit too catchy), and is made even better by the fact that the true culprit is never revealed.

Season 2 - Episode 21

While working on the final diorama for their anthropology class, Chang discovers the cache of items stolen by Troy's monkey.

Clip shows are episodes of television series that are made up of excerpts from previous episodes (to save on budget). Paradigms of Human Memory takes this sitcom staple to the next level by creating a clip show out of entirely new moments. Chang can be seen outside of the study room in the diorama, the episode introduced “six seasons and a movie”, and the Gravity montages are a shout-out to a Jeff/Annie fan video from back in 2009 (Dan Harmon paid out of pocket for the song).

Season 3 - Episode 17

When the yam the study group was growing for biology class is killed, Troy and Abed are on the case.

Basic Lupine Urology (a Dick Wolf play on words) is another flawless homage (this time to Law & Order). Leslie Hendrix (from Law & Order) was brought on to play the medical examiner, Troy wears a Spider-Man tie throughout the episode (a continuing reference to the Donald4Spiderman movement), and according to DVD commentary, the background gag (as Jeff buys a hotdog) went completely unnoticed by the crew. The fact that the crew is investigating a yam never gets old.

Season 2 - Episode 16

Pierce pretends to be dying so that he can bequeath a series of cryptic and sometimes mean-spirited gifts to his study partners, and Abed decides to shoot a documentary film of the situation.

The first of Community’s documentary episodes, Intermediate Documentary Filmmaking takes full advantage of the medium (referencing classic “mockumentary” shows such as The Office and Parks and Recreation). Pierce’s “gifts” to the study group are each very different, leading to hilarious moments (Troy and LeVar Burton), and deeper themes (Jeff’s relationship with his father).

Season 1 - Episode 23

Greendale Community College is transformed into an apocalyptic war zone when the dean promises the winner of a paintball competition priority registration, and it could fan the flames of sexual tension between Jeff and Britta.

Though its novelty may have created more hype than it really deserved, Modern Warfare is widely regarded as the episode that put Community on the map; no one expected it. The set is torn apart to create a post-apocalyptic homage to movies such as Die Hard, 28 Days Later, The Terminator, etc. The episode’s ambition (and the way that it addressed the tension between Jeff and Britta) marks a shift in the series.

Season 2 - Episode 10

The study group takes Troy out to a bar to celebrate his 21st birthday. While there, Annie assumes a new identity, Abed is propositioned by a man, one of Shirley's secrets is discovered, and Britta and Jeff fight over the "cool" bars.

Troy’s coming of age, Annie’s attempt to create a new identity, Shirley’s efforts to bury the past, and more come together to make one of Community’s most heartfelt episodes. Mixology Certification asks what it really means to become an adult.

Season 1 - Episode 21

Jeff's plan to get chicken fingers from the school cafeteria for the study group quickly evolves into a mafia movie-style endeavor with Abed calling all the shots.

Contemporary American Poultry is the first of Community’s famous homages–episodes that set the series apart from any other. This Goodfellas/Godfather spoof also sees Pierce’s efforts to coin “streets ahead” (the phrase was used in an attempt to insult Dan Harmon on Twitter).

Season 3 - Episode 8

The dean is given the task of making a Greendale commercial, but the shoot soon goes over budget and spirals out of control - with Abed documenting the experience.

Though many argue that Documentary Filmmaking: Redux beats out the original Intermediate Documentary Filmmaking, the deep dive that the episode takes into its concept can be off-putting for some viewers. However, the brief stardom of Dean Jeff, the arrival of Luis Guzman (who had his statue built back in S1), and the return of Jeff’s underwear from S1.E17 will never stop being funny.

Season 6 - Episode 12

A marriage proposal in Jeff's law class exposes a connection between the future bride and groom.

“My name is Elroy Patashnik, and from 2006 to 2009 I was addicted to encouraging white people.” Though S6 is missing nearly half of the original study group and its humor can be an acquired taste, Community’s second to last episode manages to stand with the best. Wedding Videography provides us with the last real glimpse into the dynamic between characters, and why saying goodbye may be for the best.

Season 1 - Episode 17

Jeff refuses to participate in a pool class because the teacher forces him to play in gym shorts, and the study group discovers a white version of Abed at the college.

Physical Education manages to be a high point of S1, even without an ambitious concept or homage. Abed’s Don Draper from Mad Men impression is made funnier by the fact that Alison Brie played Trudy Campbell in the show. Bonus points for the Rain Man reference and the werewolf in the decisive game’s background.

Season 4 - Episode 8

While the dean sets up a Sadie Hawkins dance, Britta puts together a competing Sophie B. Hawkins dance. Meanwhile, Abed is set up with two dates to both dances by Annie and Shirley.

S4 was missing the Community magic (to the point of being called the “gas leak year” in S5 and S6). Dan Harmon left the show, and behind-the-scenes conflicts continued to escalate. Herstory of Dance stands apart from the rest of the season as a simple, but heartfelt episode. Guest appearances by Brie Larson and Sophie B. Hawkins help.

Season 1 - Episode 19

Jeff signs up for a pottery class for an easy credit, but winds up failing the class when his jealousy of another student gets the best of him. Meanwhile, Pierce's incompetence could ruin his group's grade in boating class.

After being told by Troy to take “a pottery class or something” back in S1.E6, Jeff does just that. Beginner Pottery is a great example of S1 at its best. Both the A and B plots are solid, every character gets a chance to shine, and Tony Hale makes an appearance as the pottery teacher.

Season 3 - Episode 7

The study group helps Annie move in with Troy and Abed - except for Jeff, who lies to get out of it but is busted by dean Pelton.

Studies in Modern Movement gives us time with some of the show’s more unique pairings (Troy and Abed with Annie, Britta with Shirley, and Jeff with Dean Pelton). Each character was given a real-life Twitter account, and tweets about #AnniesMove would pop up while the episode was on air. The “Kiss From a Rose” karaoke will never get old.

Season 2 - Episode 4

Everyone in the study group except Abed is stuck in a crude 1980s space simulator as it is towed off campus, and Abed leads the team given the difficult assignment of bringing them home.

Basic Rocket Science is a quality homage to space race movies such as Apollo 13 and The Right Stuff. It also gets points for introducing Greendale’s flag (“the crossroads of ideas”). Pierce’s fit while on the simulator could be chalked up to his relationship with his father (who bears a striking resemblance to Colonel Sanders).

Season 1 - Episode 9

Jeff reluctantly agrees to be Annie's debate partner, Britta humors Pierce by letting him try hypnotherapy on her to get her to stop smoking, and the gang begins to think that Abed's films may predict their future.

Debate 109 is about when Community began to find its footing as a series. While the episode’s A plot is great–and Abed’s films are hilarious–the hypnosis B plot is lacking. However, the episode gets points for kicking off the Jeff and Annie pairing.

Season 2 - Episode 24

When the study group learns that there's a sinister plot behind the paintball tournament, they unite the remaining players to defeat the enemy.

This Star Wars homage serves as the second part of A Fistful of Paintballs and the S2 finale. While For a Few Paintballs More is a solid episode, it doesn’t stand on its own as well as its first part. Pay attention to the raising of the City College flag if you want an extra laugh.

Season 2 - Episode 17

Anticipating a visit from the vice president, dean Pelton holds an election for student body president. An idealistic Annie decides to run, but Jeff decides to run against her to spite her and show how stupid politics can be.

From the candidate IDs to Annie’s ACB speech, Intro to Political Science is full of small details. The scrolling news feed during Troy and Abed’s commentary includes the following messages: “western theme panic” (a reference to the season finale), “chicken finger shortage continues” (S1.E21), and “Professor Slater still missing” (calling out her disappearance following S1). The episode also predicted Biden’s presidency.

Season 3 - Episode 2

When another overachieving student steals Annie's idea for a Model U.N. club, the Study Group comes to her rescue. Meanwhile, new security cop Chang tries to stop Britta's last-ditch efforts to create a campus disturbance.

Geography of Global Conflict is yet another great example of how Community can take a fairly grounded premise (Model U.N.) and blow it out of proportion (with hilarious moments and sweet character development).

Season 3 - Episode 19

When Abed claims the Dean has been replaced by a doppelgänger he is forced to take psychiatric treatment. Naturally, the rest of the study group joins him at his session with Dr. Heidi.

Curriculum Unavailable is the second episode to take the “clip show” format. Though some argue that the episode includes better moments than Paradigms of Human Memory, it’s missing the novelty that made the original stand out. However, the asylum twist deserves a special mention (with Dan Harmon’s voice over Garrett’s character).

Season 6 - Episode 11

When a secret game of paintball occurs on campus, Frankie threatens to expel those who participate.

While Modern Espionage is arguably the weakest of Community’s paintball episodes (ignoring S4), it’s still great. One highlight is Dean Pelton’s elevator scene–a clear homage to the Russo brothers’ recent success with Captain America: The Winter Soldier. The episode’s silver bullets and mannequin finale are also a reference to The Man with the Golden Gun (a James Bond film). Seeing Jeff’s paintball fame is a nice touch.

Season 1 - Episode 3

Britta comes between Abed and his father when she signs him up for a film class, and Jeff risks failing a class unless he "seizes the day."

The third episode of the series–Introduction to Film–is where Community first demonstrated its ability to combine ridiculous comedy with more tender moments. We get to learn more about Abed’s relationship with his father and watch Professor Whitman pretend to be from Dead Poets Society. Seize the day!

Season 1 - Episode 7

Will Jeff blow off Annie's Halloween party and sell out his other classmates in order to get a chance to score with a sexy professor?

People have mixed feelings about the relationship between Jeff and Professor Slater, but everyone seems to agree that Introduction to Statistics was solid (maybe the best episode up to that point). It introduced holiday episodes, Britta’s off-the-wall costumes, Abed’s Batman (shout out to the bloopers), and more.

Season 2 - Episode 7

Britta, Annie and Shirley discover that Abed is skilled at insulting the mean girls, so they use him to their advantage. Meanwhile, Troy and Jeff find a secret garden with a trampoline, which they use for relaxation.

In Aerodynamics of Gender, Jeff and Troy find an otherworldly paradise in the form of a trampoline (a reference to The Secret Garden). The episode also sees Abed become the Terminator of Mean Girls insults, with his HUD revealing future details for the show (the paintball sequel, the Christmas episode, the blanket fort, etc.). Matt Walsh and Hilary Duff’s guest appearances are just the cherry on top.

Season 5 - Episode 2

Jeff blows off his new teaching job at Greendale until Annie signs up for his class and forces him to teach, and Abed loses his religion when he takes a course about whether Nicolas Cage is good or bad.

Introduction to Teaching showed us that Community really was back for S5. Abed’s deep dive into Nicolas Cage is hilarious, and it’s nice to get a glimpse of Jeff enjoying his job as a teacher. When rioting students enter the cafeteria, a cork-board can be seen falling down from the wall; this same cork-board becomes the focus of S5.E6.

Season 5 - Episode 6

Annie and Hickey fight Greendale bureaucracy to install a bulletin board, Abed learns a lesson about sign language, and the others discover the challenges of planning a properly sensitive school dance.

Analysis of Cork-Based Networking may not be the best that Community has to offer, but “bear down for midterms” gets funnier with every rewatch. The episode finally calls Abed out for his spontaneous “relationships”, “Levar Burton and non-celebrity companion captured by pirates in the Gulf of Mexico” flashes on the news, and Paget Brewster is featured before her return as Frankie in S6.

Season 2 - Episode 1

Jeff and Britta engage in a dangerous game of relationship one-upmanship in response to Britta's embarrassing moment at the end of the previous school year, which could cause the entire study group to fall apart.

“I'm hoping we can move away from the soapy, relationship-y stuff and into bigger, fast-paced, self-contained escapades.” Anthropology 101 made it clear that Community had no intention of going mainstream, as any sort of romantic pairings were cut. Instead, we see Jeff getting strangled by Betty White. Running through the study group’s rooms at the episode’s beginning is nice (Troy wears his Donald4Spiderman pajamas).

Season 3 - Episode 9

Shirley teaches Jeff foosball so that he can beat a team of cocky, obnoxious Germans, while Annie creates an overly elaborate ruse so that she doesn't have to tell Abed that she broke his special edition The Dark Knight DVD.

Foosball and Nocturnal Vigilantism sees the return of Abed’s Batman, the rare pairing of Jeff and Shirley, and an anime sequence… what more could you want? Jeff and Shirley’s resolution is heartwarming.

Season 2 - Episode 2

Jeff blows off the study group and their break-dancing competition to hang out with his old co-workers at the law firm, but Annie tries to prove that one of them is the person who turned him in and got him fired.

From the chloroform scene to the study group’s gradual degeneration at the party, Accounting for Lawyers is full of good moments. The cupping sound that can be heard when Jeff shakes his former boss’s hand gets louder with every rewatch.

Season 3 - Episode 10

A holiday-themed parody of "Glee" in which Abed tries to persuade each member of the Study Group to join the glee club so that they can qualify for regionals.

“Oh, Britta’s in this?” Some people’s aversion to musicals is the only reason why Regional Holiday Music didn’t make A-tier. Community is known for picking on Glee, but this episode takes it to a new extreme by parodying Invasion of the Body Snatchers. Every song is solid, and getting to hear Childish Gambino rap as Troy is gold.

Season 6 - Episode 13

As their sixth year at Greendale draws to a close, Abed asks everyone to imagine pitching a TV show about what they would do in season seven.

Emotional Consequences of Broadcast Television was the perfect finale to Community (at least until the movie). However, both its humor and heart depend on the connection that the audience has with the show, meaning some viewers may not enjoy the episode (flag for language). Its meta writing builds to the point of addressing the audience directly in what may be the best end tag of the series.

Season 3 - Episode 5

During her pre-Halloween party, Britta gets everyone to tell a spooky story so that she can find out which member of the study group scored out as a psychopath on their psychology profiles.

Horror Fiction in Seven Spooky Steps introduced Michael Haggins’ “Daybreak” (which can be heard in S3.E9, S3.E11, S3.E13, S3.E14, S3.E16, S4.E4, S4.E5, S4.E9, and S6.E11) and delivered on the long-running “Beetlejuice” gag. The episode also sees Britta reading “Warren Piece” (a mistaken version of Tolstoy’s “War and Peace”), hinting at her misguided sense of intellectualism.

Season 2 - Episode 6

The study group is left to fend for themselves by Dean Pelton during a rabies outbreak due to tainted food at Greendale's Halloween party.

This ABBA-fueled zombie apocalypse in the library is made even better by the fact that it actually happens in the show’s canon (it’s not a dream sequence or an alternate reality). Though Epidemiology is a fan favorite, it doesn’t reach the heights of other A-tier episodes.

Season 3 - Episode 20

The study group must help Pierce win a video game so that he can claim his inheritance from his father, but somebody may be out to ensure that he loses.

Digital Estate Planning is a great homage to 8-bit games and the amount of effort that must have gone into animation earns the episode a top spot on its own. Seeing Breaking Bad’s Giancarlo Esposito make a guest appearance as Pierce’s half brother is great. However, sitting through an episode that is almost entirely pixelated may be harder for some viewers than others.

Season 5 - Episode 5

As a going-away present to Troy before his around-the-world trip, Abed sets up a high-stakes game of "Hot Lava" at the college, but Britta suspects that the game is just masking his real feelings about Troy's departure.

"Did I accidentally listen to 'Come Sail Away' by Styx again?" While Geothermal Escapism is often hailed as one of Community’s greatest episodes, it’s just a bit too weird at times (to the point of being cringey). However, Troy’s farewell really shines. The Childish Tycoon (Pierce’s boat), is a reference to Donald Glover's rap career as Childish Gambino.

Season 5 - Episode 8

When a new app is beta tested on Greendale students, the campus soon descends into a dystopian society where status is determined by app standing.

App Development and Condiments is another episode that can rank a bit too high at times (though we all wish the MeowMeowBeenz app were real). While the episode is a great dystopian parody (and social commentary) it's simply too weird to watch with most audiences.

Season 2 - Episode 11

When Abed wakes up to discover that everything is in stop motion animation, Professor Duncan and the study group help him try to discover the true meaning of Christmas.

The fact that Community took the time to make an entire episode in stop motion (à la Rudolph the Red-Nosed Reindeer) is amazing. While Abed's Uncontrollable Christmas can be a bit odd to sit through if you don’t know what to expect, its creative medium and heartfelt message land the episode high up on most lists.

Season 2 - Episode 19

Jeff's "Pulp Fiction"-themed surprise party for Abed is spoiled when Abed tells him over dinner that he's done with pop culture.

Critical Film Studies is another popular episode, building up to a Pulp Fiction homage while actually mirroring My Dinner with Andre the entire time. It’s nice to see Jeff get more time with Abed again, and having Abed actually make it onto the set of Cougar Town in its second season is a nice touch. However, the episode can be a too meta for some audiences.

Season 3 - Episode 1

Jeff's plan to keep Pierce out of the Study Group backfires when he gets kicked out instead, Abed copes with bad news about Cougar Town, and Dean Pelton's authority is challenged by another Greendale dean.

“We're gonna seem like a mainstream dream… and be appealing to all mankind!” Biology 101 is a solid season opener (if a bit extreme at times). However, the real humor is found in the behind-the-scenes context that not all viewers have (Community’s refusal to go mainstream). The entire opening song might as well be a giant middle finger to NBC (and their studio notes on how to improve the show).

Season 3 - Episode 21

The Study Group, now known as the "Greendale Seven," launches a plot to rescue the real dean Pelton and end Chang's reign of terror over Greendale.

The First Chang Dynasty is a fun homage to heist movies such as Ocean’s 11. However, the idea of Chang taking over the entire school with an army of adolescents can be a bit much for some viewers (even in Community).

Season 4 - Episode 11

When Annie and Shirley learn that Leonard is actually holding the spot as the class valedictorian, they join forces to bring him down.

Basic Human Anatomy stands with Herstory of Dance as a high point of S4 (and it gets points for finally ending Troy and Britta’s relationship). Seeing Troy and Abed swap bodies (à la Freaky Friday) is great, and seeing Annie fall for the dean is even better.

Season 3 - Episode 16

After sending Troy and Britta on a date, Annie volunteers to keep Abed company in his Dreamatorium.

Virtual Systems Analysis is weird, which can turn some viewers off. However, the episode is creative, tender, and dives into the relationships between several members of the study group.

Season 5 - Episode 10

The group proposes a "sequel" to Dungeons & Dragons night when they need to get a skeptical professor Hickey to bond with his estranged son.

While it was nice to see the dean participating and get a cameo appearance from David Cross, Advanced Advanced Dungeons & Dragons is missing the novelty of the original (though some argue that this episode was executed better).

Season 2 - Episode 5

Shirley asks Abed to make a film about Jesus, but he quickly angers Shirley by turning it into a self-indulgent mess. Meanwhile, Pierce begins blowing off the study group to hang out with the "hipsters".

Messianic Myths and Ancient Peoples is a meta episode about a meta movie created by the show’s most meta character. While the episode is completely unique and some love it, its weirdness (and portrayal of Abed as the Messiah) can be off-putting.

Season 6 - Episode 7

An ex-boyfriend persuades Britta to join a guerilla marketing campaign.

Advanced Safety Features is a solid episode of Community. It’s nice to see Britta reunite with Rick (Subway), and Billy Zane’s guest appearance will never stop being funny. However, what really makes this episode great is the context. Honda payed for product placement, and Community went overboard by creating an episode about guerilla marketing (while marketing Honda to the audience).

Season 1 - Episode 10

Jeff hangs out with Senor Chang to get out of an essay, angering the rest of the group. Meanwhile, Abed and Troy search for their missing lab mouse, and Pierce helps Shirley out with a presentation.

The "Somewhere Out There" episode, Environmental Science is packed with hilarious lines and heartfelt moments, continuing S1’s momentum. It’s also nice to meet Chang’s wife (who returns in S4.E6).

Season 6 - Episode 1

Dean Pelton hires an administrative consultant to organize things at Greendale.

This episode features the return of Greendale’s greatest class: Ladders (seen back in S3.E19). From its very first episode, S6 felt different from the rest of the series. Viewers who grow to love S6’s distinct humor, heart, and cast will love this episode; others may not.

Season 1 - Episode 2

Jeff takes steps to ensure that Britta will be his partner in Spanish class, but winds up with Pierce instead.

Spanish 101 is a prime example of what makes S1 different from the rest of Community (for better or for worse). The premise hinges on Jeff’s pursuit of Britta, certain characters seem different, and the overall tone just feels more mainstream (though the episode does get points for introducing Chang).

Season 3 - Episode 3

Chaos ensues when the Study Group tries to divide itself up into biology lab partners, and Chang appoints himself security "detective," trying to solve a mystery that is only in his head.

From Profesor Kane’s Lego speech to the group’s treatment of Todd, Competitive Ecology has its moments. It puts the characters’ negative traits on full display (which some love and others don’t). The image above is a chart showing what the anonymous ballot results from the episode could have been. “No offense, Todd.”

Season 6 - Episode 4

Dean Pelton is asked to lie; Chang auditions for a stage adaptation of "The Karate Kid."

Queer Studies & Advanced Waxing is one of the few times that we get to see Chang succeed, and this leads Annie to grow as well. Jason Mantzoukas’ cameo as a Whiplash-esque director is hilarious, and Frankie’s comment on the missing IT lady becomes even funnier when you realize that Paget Brewster played the IT lady back in S5.E6.

Season 1 - Episode 13

The gang decides not to let an obnoxious student from their Spanish class into the study group. The dean appoints Jeff as editor of the student paper, where Annie is working on a story that could expose the dean as a racist.

Guest appearances from Jack Black and Owen Wilson are reason enough to watch Investigative Journalism. While the episode has its moments (rolling the title early), the humor can be a bit too cringey at times.

Season 3 - Episode 11

Pierce comes to Shirley with a business proposal just as she's about to get married to Andre. Meanwhile, Troy and Abed try to act "normal" for the wedding, Britta discovers a new skill, and Jeff struggles with his toast.

“This may shock you Annie, but I come come from a long line of wives & mothers.” Urban Matrimony and the Sandwich Arts isn’t the best of S3, but watching Troy and Abed pretend to be normal and seeing Britta become a wedding planner is hilarious.

Season 2 - Episode 15

Jeff hangs out with Professor Duncan after a fight with the study group, but Chang tags along and starts a party. Meanwhile, Abed and Troy fight for a librarian's affection, and Britta goes to a dance with a girl she thinks is a lesbian.

Early 21st Century Romanticism sets up S2.E16 with Pierce’s pill addiction, gives us “Heartburns”, and introduces Magnitude (who also played Lee Jordan in the Harry Potter films). It’s sweet to see Jeff realize that he needs the study group.

Season 1 - Episode 8

When Jeff loses his home and his car, he is forced to move into Abed's dorm room. Troy has a big date, but why is a jealous Annie trying to help? Meanwhile, Pierce becomes the keyboardist for Vaughn's band.

If you love the Vaughn’s music, you’ll love Home Economics. "Some Worries" (the name of Vaughn's band) is a reference to his last line in S1.E4, and is seen again on a sticker in S3.E9.

Season 1 - Episode 1

An ex-lawyer is forced to return to community college to get a degree. However, he tries to use the skills he learned as a lawyer to get the answers to all his tests and pick up on a sexy woman in his Spanish class.

This episode was the beginning of Community, and did an incredible job of introducing every member of the cast (and the show’s premise) in just 20 minutes. However, the Pilot’s value really comes from its relationship to the rest of the series, and it doesn’t stand on its own as well as other episodes.

Season 5 - Episode 1

Having failed in his second go-round as a lawyer, and unable to gather evidence for a potential lawsuit against Greendale, Jeff tries to convince the other former members of the Study Group to sue the school.

Similar to S1.E1, this episode's value is tied to its relationship with the rest of the series. Repilot’s role was to breath new life into Community after a disastrous fourth season. The episode manages to bring the study group back together again (albeit with a slightly darker tone), but doesn’t stand on its own very well.

Season 3 - Episode 13

When Britta falls for the human embodiment of the Subway sandwich chain, Shirley and Pierce hope to use the situation to their advantage.

Getting to see more John Goodman and the beginning of Britta’s relationship with Subway is always fun. However, Digital Exploration of Interior Design doesn’t stand alone nearly as well as Pillows and Blankets (the episode that it builds up to). The humor behind the episode’s title and the Subway executive’s refusal to stand up goes over too many heads (thought it’s probably for the best).

Season 3 - Episode 18

A fellow student has died and Biology class has been canceled, meaning everyone has to retake it in summer school. When the memorial service turns into a riot, Chang is granted special authoritative powers.

Watching the “Greendale Seven” start a riot in the lunchroom following the death of Starburns, only to be taken out by Chang’s army of children is hilarious (if a bit ridiculous). When the study group is in the infirmary following the riot, Abed has a towel over his face because Danny Pudi was unavailable (due to the birth of his twin daughters).

Season 1 - Episode 25

Britta and Professor Slater fight for the affection of Jeff. Meanwhile, Troy is confused when Abed doesn't invite him to move into his dorm room.

Pascal's Triangle Revisited throws S1’s love triangle out the window, ending the season with Jeff and Annie. This is refreshing, as watching Britta and Professor Slater fight over Jeff can be uncomfortable. The episode also introduces the traumatic incident that Britta had with a “transient in a dinosaur costume” at her 11th birthday party (a gag that would be revisited in S2.E6, S3.E19, and S3.E22).

Season 6 - Episode 2

Jeff and Frankie ask an inventor for help extracting Dean Pelton from his virtual reality world.

"What's so funny? I mean... besides everything?" Some people love the way that Lawnmower Maintenance & Postnatal Care introduced Britta’s parents, others hate it. But nobody can dislike the dean’s time in virtual reality.

Season 6 - Episode 3

The Greendale gang goes on the offensive when a rival college releases an attack ad.

While the A-plot of Basic Crisis Room Decorum isn’t incredible, and it's hard to believe that Annie would transfer after so long, the dean’s text exchanges with “Jeff” make the episode worth watching all on their own.

Season 1 - Episode 24

Annie uses inside information about Señor Chang to get him fired so that the study group will be forced to stay together and take Spanish again. Meanwhile, Troy discovers that he has a natural skill for plumbing.

English as a Second Language is a good episode, and it gets points for introducing Troy's innate talent as a repairman (in a reference to Good Will Hunting). However, it was sad to lose Sr. Chang.

Season 1 - Episode 12

Shirley throws a Christmas party for the gang and much to her surprise learns her classmates come from various religious backgrounds. Meanwhile, Jeff is challenged by the school bully and faces the reality that he may fail Spanish 101.

Comparative Religion did a solid job of introducing us to each of the characters’ religious backgrounds. The episode’s bully is played by Anthony Michael Hall, who played Brian Johnson in The Breakfast Club (referenced heavily in S1.E1) and Chevy Chase's son Rusty Griswold in National Lampoon's Vacation.

Season 3 - Episode 22

Jeff faces an old nemesis as Shirley and Pierce square off in Greendale court, Abed succumbs to his evil alter ego, and Troy decides to meet his destiny at air conditioning school.

Though Introduction to Finality served its purpose as a potential ending to Community (with Shirley’s business, the darkest timeline, the rivalry between Jeff and Alan, and a closing montage), the show’s continuation can make the episode feel less memorable.

Season 2 - Episode 12

Annie lobbies for her new crush, who is also Jeff's rival, to join the study group, but a jealous Jeff counters with Chang. Meanwhile, Shirley announces that she's pregnant, but she doesn't know that it could be Chang's.

In Asian Population Studies, Andre (played by Malcolm Jamal Warner) mentions that his sweater was a gift from his dad. This is a reference to the colorful sweaters that Bill Cosby wore on The Cosby Show (where Warner played his son). The scene where Jeff runs through the rain is a classic example of Community refusing to do the expected.

Season 1 - Episode 15

When Annie starts hanging out with Britta's ex-boyfriend Vaughn, Britta and Jeff vow to protect her. Meanwhile, Pierce feels left out at Troy and Abed's movie night.

Though the “sexual prospect glances” bit is one of the best moments in S1, the A-plot of Romantic Expressionism isn’t the best.

Season 1 - Episode 20

Britta's April Fool's Day prank has disastrous consequences, but she refuses to confess that she was responsible. Annie and Shirley temporarily work as security guards, but both of them want to be the "bad cop."

The Science of Illusion is a nice homage to buddy cop movies, but it doesn’t stand out much as an episode.

Season 1 - Episode 16

Jeff sets out to fix his relationship with Britta after things get awkward with a drunken phone call. Meanwhile, Annie and Shirley make plans to humiliate Señor Chang in order to defend Troy and Pierce's dignity.

Communication Studies is a great example of the drunk dial sitcom staple (and started the Beetlejuice gag), but it doesn’t pop the way that other episodes do.

Season 6 - Episode 10

While on a road trip to get rid of a Greendale artifact, Abed turns a situation into a feature film.

Though some people love Basic RV Repair and Palmistry (watching Abed try to flash back and forward is fun), the episode can fall flat if you don't have a tolerance for meta humor.

Season 1 - Episode 14

Jeff and Shirley bond by making fun of Britta's new hippie boyfriend, which doesn't go well for any involved. Meanwhile, Troy and Abed become pawns in Annie's psychology experiment.

Social Psychology introduces Vaughn, and has a sweet moment between Abed and Annie, but doesn’t stand out it many other ways.

Season 1 - Episode 11

Annie helps the dean put on an "STD Fair," but doesn't want to perform an embarrassing demonstration. Meanwhile Jeff finds a companion for a double date with Pierce, and Troy finds out that Abed is a better athlete than him.

Politics of Human Sexuality gets points for introducing us to Officer Cackowski. About nine months after this episode aired, Abed can be seen delivering a baby in the background of S2.E3 (a reference to his announcement in this episode).

Season 5 - Episode 13

When the group learns about Greendale's first dean, they embark on a mission to find his old computer lab; Subway enlists Chang to spy on the group as part of its plan to take over the school.

The second episode of S5’s two-part finale, Basic Sandwich is a fun Goonies homage that took a very meta perspective on the show’s imminent cancellation. While watching Richie read minds was something that we never knew we needed, the episode brings up too many points that are never touched on again (Borchert, Jeff and Annie, the school board’s vendetta, etc.).

Season 5 - Episode 12

After a liability audit, the Save Greendale Committee discovered that they have, in fact, saved Greendale - until they discover that as a result of the successful audit, the school is being sold to Subway.

Basic Story was upfront about Community’s potential cancellation, which led to some fun moments (if a bit too meta). However, both this episode and its second part (Basic Sandwich) don’t stand well on their own, and seem to fall flat.

Season 6 - Episode 6

A hacker threatens to publish emails unless a scheduled appearance by a racist comic is canceled.

From Jeff emailing astronauts to Chang’s ranking of Annie and Britta (referencing Britta’s glasses in S4.E1), Basic Email Security is full of fun confessions (though the episode’s ethics and logic can be off-putting for some viewers). It’s worth noting that the lunch lady’s emails include “Buzz Hickey Memorial Services”.

Season 5 - Episode 9

Whilst cleaning the storage room Jeff, Hickey and Shirley find a pile of unused hidden textbooks and plot a way to make the best out of it. Meanwhile, Annie and Abed are trying to find a new roommate.

While it was clever to feature Vince Gilligan and Jonathan Banks in a parody of Breaking Bad (and it’s nice to see Brie Larson with Abed again), the real highlight of VCR Maintenance and Educational Publishing is Dean Pelton’s Payday Apology Rap in the cold open.

Season 3 - Episode 15

While Britta struggles to stay away from her old carny boyfriend, Jeff becomes obsessed with their relationship. Meanwhile, the dean stops by Troy and Abed's to recruit Troy for the dean of the school of refrigeration.

Origins of Vampire Mythology is a fun episode (with good emotional payoff at the end), but it struggles to stand out when compared with the rest. “Read the banana, Britta.”

Season 2 - Episode 3

Pierce's religion convinces him that the spirit of his dead mother is captured in a cheap lava lamp, Jeff has a crisis of faith when he learns he has high cholesterol, and Britta and Annie try to raise money for the oil spill.

While the A-Plot of The Psychology of Letting Go is good (and builds up S5.E4 as Troy finally manages to “eat a ghost”), the real highlight of the episode is Abed’s delivery of a baby in the background (which is referenced again in S2.E22).

Season 2 - Episode 13

Pierce bribes Annie into getting him a bigger role in her anti-drug play, Jeff gets in trouble when he sends a lewd text message from Britta's phone, and Shirley rejects Chang's attempts to be friendly to her.

“I’m flying higher than I ever have thanks to not drugs.” Though Celebrity Pharmacology has its moments and one-liners, the episode falls flat overall (especially when compared with the rest of S2).

Season 1 - Episode 18

It's Family Day at Greendale. Pierce tries to build a relationship with his stepdaughter, but she has eyes for Jeff instead. Britta gets in trouble with Troy's Nana, and Shirley's kids cause problems for Abed's father.

While Basic Genealogy definitely isn’t the best of S1, it’s a simple episode that has great moments and brings Jeff’s relationship with Professor Slater to an end. Just about every Chang moment in the episosde is gold.

Season 1 - Episode 6

Jeff talks Troy into playing for the college's football team, sparking Annie's anger. Pierce helps the dean come up with a new mascot, and Britta tries to become Shirley's bathroom friend.

Football, Feminism and You has its moments, but seeing Troy become involved in football (something that’s never really touched on again) can isolate the episode from the rest of the series.

Season 1 - Episode 5

Señor Chang is on the hunt for a cheater and threatens to fail the entire class if the culprit doesn't come forward. When someone confesses, Jeff comes to their defense. Meanwhile, Annie recruits Pierce to write the new school song.

Advanced Criminal Law is unfortunately one of S1’s more forgettable episodes (though it does get points for setting up Luis Guzman’s arrival in S3.E8).

Season 1 - Episode 14

Jeff runs into problems when the study group and the dean discover that he's seeing a professor, while Troy and Britta discover each other's secret that they are both taking dance classes.

Interpretive Dance makes Jeff and Professor Slater official, and gives us time with Britta and Troy (two relationships that people seem to have mixed feelings about). Overall, the episode sits near the bottom of S1.

Season 5 - Episode 7

Jeff helps Duncan score a date with Britta, and Abed has a Hickey encounter on his way to the movie theater.

Bondage and Beta Male Sexuality gives us more time with Professor Duncan and does its best to tackle Troy’s absence, but this just makes it all the more noticeable. The “Old Timey Photo Club” gag was great, however, and featured several writers and small cameos (including Annie's brother, Dan Harmon, and Chris McKenna).

Season 6 - Episode 8

The committee, along with a movie producer named Maury, help Abed complete his unfinished film.

Intro to Recycled Cinema is an episode that gets a good amount of love and a good amount of hate. Very weird but very funny.

Season 3 - Episode 6

Pierce gets a visit from his bigoted father after his wipes become a hit in the gay community, and the dean of the School of Refrigeration recruits Troy to become his student.

“Pierce, I just want to say… sorry for killing your dad.” While the episode has its moments, Advanced Gay sits near the bottom of S3. Cornelius Hawthorne’s racism can be off-putting and Troy’s experience with the AC repair school annex is a bit too weird.

Season 2 - Episode 22

As the semester draws to a close, the anthropology class final is disrupted when Shirley goes into labor one month ahead of schedule.

Applied Anthropology and Culinary Arts is a bottle episode (though Pierce buying Troy and Abed’s handshake is great). Note that when Professor Duncan messes around behind the school flag, he is actually pulling the fire alarm (which doesn’t work).

Season 4 - Episode 2

The study group's Halloween plans are put on hold when Pierce locks himself in the panic room of his "haunted" mansion.

While Paranormal Parentage still has that “off” feeling that permeates S4, it manages to be a passable episode. Troy and Abed’s Calvin and Hobbes costumes are a reference to S1.E17, and Pierce’s line about ghosts not being fire is a callback to Chang’s line in S3.E21 (about fire not being a ghost).

Season 4 - Episode 5

While Jeff and Britta spend Thanksgiving with Jeff's newly-discovered father and brother, the rest of the Study Group tries to escape from Shirley's dysfunctional family Thanksgiving celebration.

Cooperative Escapism in Familial Relations is another passable S4 episode. It’s nice to see a moment of vulnerability from Jeff and meet his father (James Brolin and Adam Devine were both great). In the episode’s cold open, the dean is seen wearing Jeff’s cowboy outfit from S1.E7, S2.E21, S2.E23, and S2.E24.

Season 3 - Episode 12

The members of the Study Group take jobs as celebrity impersonators to pay off Abed's debt to a celebrity impersonator business, but can Jeff be Ryan Seacrest without his fragile ego spiral out of control?

“Even his shadow… look at his shadow!” Contemporary Impressionists isn’t one of S3’s best, but is still fun to watch (though some people don't like the way that Abed treats the rest of the study group).

Season 2 - Episode 20

Jeff suspects something isn't quite right when an attractive classmate gets engaged to marry Pierce.

Competitive Wine Tasting gives Pierce the spotlight and lets us see Professor Garrity again, but doesn’t deliver much else.

Season 2 - Episode 18

Jeff uses Shirley's parental rights waiver against Chang as a way to get Chang out of the apartment, while Britta is afraid to tell Abed and Troy that their new friend is a war criminal.

Unfortunately, Custody Law and Eastern European Diplomacy just falls flat when compared with the rest of S2 (unless you really like watching Chang pretend to be Mr. Rogers).

Season 4 - Episode 12

Abed's research into the study group's history uncovers an overlooked series of chance encounters that led each of them to Greendale.

Heroic Origins is a guilty pleasure episode. While it can be fun to watch, the episode undercuts Community’s central theme and retcons several points that were previously established (though seeing Annie in the background of the courthouse as Greendale's "Citizen of the Month" for several months in a row is a nice touch).

Season 4 - Episode 10

Jeff organizes a lovely holiday gathering at his apartment but Annie secretly invites Professor Cornwallis, to get in his good graces.

Though some defend Intro to Knots, most agree that the episode was a fun concept executed poorly.

Season 4 - Episode 3

At the Inspector SpaceTime Convention, Abed finds a new "soulmate," Jeff discovers he looks like one of the show's villains, Shirley and Pierce join a focus group, and Annie pretends to be Jeff's wife.

Conventions of Space and Time is often cited as an example of how S4 fell short, but the episode has its charm. The deleted scene where Jeff agrees with the bartender does bring the episode full circle.

Season 6 - Episode 5

A prisoner attends Greendale via telerobot; Britta tries to get around one of Annie's rules.

Laws of Robotics & Party Rights gets points for predicting online school, but not much else. It’s interesting that the episodes “antagonist” is named Willy (like Jeff’s father and half-brother).

Season 6 - Episode 9

When a clever clever con man, Professor DeSalvo, uses his class to cheat the study group, they enlist Jeff to get revenge. The situation goes awry as Britta and Dean Pelton get involved in Jeff and Professor DeSalvo's scamming competition.

Grifting 101’s poor usage of Matt Berry as a guest star (along with its lackluster plot) cause the episode to fall flat.

Season 5 - Episode 11

The entire group becomes part of the cast of a classic "G.I. Joe" cartoon from the 1980s, which turns out to be part of Jeff's coping mechanism as he deals with a monumental life event.

G.I. Jeff is a poor attempt at replicating Abed's Uncontrollable Christmas by retconning Jeff’s age. While the animation sets it apart, the episode just doesn’t do it for most (unless you watched G.I. Joe).

Season 4 - Episode 9

Dean Pelton prompts the study group to use puppets to relive the traumatic events of a recent adventure.

The infamous puppet episode. While Intro to Felt Surrogacy gets more hate than it deserves, it’s a perfect example of S4 doing a concept episode for the sake of doing a concept episode (though it is nice to see Sara Bareilles make an appearance).

Season 4 - Episode 4

The Study Group loses access to the study room to the German foosballers, and Chang returns to campus, claiming to be suffering from "Changnesia."

Alternative History of the German Invasion isn’t great. It has fun moments and lines, but is another S4 episode that’s just missing something.

Season 4 - Episode 1

While the Dean creates a Hunger Games-style competition for admission into a History of Ice Cream class, the final class Jeff needs to graduate and a fear of graduation causes Abed to retreat to his happy place.

History 101 was the beginning of S4, parodies for the sake of parodies, and Troy’s relationship with Britta. That should speak for itself.

Season 4 - Episode 6

Abed makes a new documentary chronicling Greendale's effort to get a research grant for "Changnesia," as well as Jeff's efforts to prove that Chang is faking his disease.

Not many are fans of the “Changnesia” plotline in S4, and Advanced Documentary Filmmaking revolves around it (though Partner and Houlihan are fun).

Season 4 - Episode 13

A plan to celebrate Jeff's early graduation is unexpectedly disrupted by the study group's evil doppelgangers from the Darkest Timeline.

Advanced Introduction to Finality can be hard to watch. While the episode includes the darkest timeline, paintball, and Jeff’s graduation, it feels forced, and would have been a poor note to end the series on.

Season 4 - Episode 7

Dean Pelton and Annie try to land a "whale" student while Jeff is assigned to keep Pierce occupied. Meanwhile, Troy and Shirley take a physical education education course, and Abed creates a fraternity to annoy the dean.

There just isn’t much to say about Economics of Marine Biology. This episode may be Community’s most forgettable.

Season 1 - Episode 22

Shirley gets Pierce kicked out the study group, Jeff and Britta plot revenge on a group of high schoolers who make fun of them for attending community college, and Troy helps Abed accomplish some of his movie-clichéd social goals.

Though not necessarily the worst, The Art of Discourse is hands-down the hardest episode of Community to watch. “Schmitty.”
